A new paper rarely appears in isolation; it inherits conceptual structure from prior work while introducing some degree of novelty. Despite this, common research-evaluation tools still rely primarily on relational proxies such as citation counts, journal impact measures, and author-level indices. These indicators are useful for large-scale monitoring, but they do not directly quantify \emph{what} content is inherited and \emph{what} content is newly contributed.

\subsection{Limits of current evaluation tools}
Traditional bibliometrics conflate visibility with contribution. Citation volume is sensitive to field size, publication velocity, review-article effects, and strategic citation behavior \cite{Mingers2015,Callaham2002}. Author-level summaries such as the $h$-index can reward sustained productivity, yet they remain agnostic to semantic distinctiveness and to uneven contribution across papers \cite{Medoff2006,Wang2014}. Altmetrics broaden the signal surface, but they typically track attention dynamics rather than intellectual dependence \cite{Bornmann2014,Nath2021}. As a result, existing tools are limited when the central question is whether a publication materially extends knowledge.

A content-aware evaluation requires separating two components:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \emph{Inherited knowledge}: semantic content attributable to cited predecessors.
    \item \emph{Novel contribution}: semantic residual not explained by those predecessors.
\end{itemize}

This separation enables richer analyses: identifying papers that primarily consolidate prior findings versus papers that introduce substantial conceptual additions; tracing pathways of knowledge transfer between fields; and monitoring shifts in research trajectories that may correspond to emerging paradigms \cite{Kuhn1962}.

\subsection{Genetic perspective on scientific evolution}
To operationalize this decomposition, we model publications in a shared semantic space using transformer-based scientific-text embeddings \cite{Beltagy2019}. For each focal paper, the embedding is approximated as a weighted combination of embeddings from its cited papers (its ``parents'') plus a residual term. The parent weights define edge-specific \emph{inheritance factors}, while the residual defines the paper-level \emph{contribution factor}. Conceptually, this induces a pseudo-genetic interpretation of science: papers have multiple parents, inherit unevenly, and express innovations as mutation-like residuals.

When applied across a citation network, this formulation produces a directed acyclic structure with interpretable inheritance strengths on edges and contribution magnitude on nodes. The result is not merely another ranking score; it is a representation of how ideas are composed, propagated, and transformed.

\subsection{Contributions}
This paper makes four main contributions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We formalize inheritance and contribution as a constrained semantic decomposition over citation neighborhoods.
    \item We present an end-to-end pipeline for data acquisition, embedding, graph construction, and factor estimation on a medium-scale corpus.
    \item We evaluate the framework against conventional bibliometric baselines (citation counts, PageRank-derived influence, and author-index proxies) and analyze where content-aware signals diverge from popularity signals.
    \item We demonstrate qualitative and quantitative use cases, including lineage tracing, cross-disciplinary transfer analysis, and early detection of structurally novel publications.
\end{itemize}
