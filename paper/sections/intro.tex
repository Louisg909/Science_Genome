Scientific progress emerges through reuse, adaptation, and recombination of prior ideas. Citation networks record part of this process, but citation counts and centrality metrics alone do not reveal how much intellectual content is inherited from predecessors versus newly contributed by a paper.

This work formalizes a content-aware framework for that distinction. Each paper is represented in a shared semantic space and approximated as a constrained mixture of cited-parent embeddings plus a residual term. The mixture weights encode lineage-specific inheritance, while the residual captures contribution not attributable to cited predecessors.

\subsection{Problem focus}
The framework is designed to support analysis of:
\begin{itemize}
    \item idea lineage and parent dependence,
    \item persistence and transformation of semantic traits,
    \item divergence and convergence across branches,
    \item and cross-domain transfer of methods or concepts.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Research stance}
The objective is analytical interpretation, not causal proof of influence. Inheritance and contribution factors are evidential signals that require uncertainty-aware reading and expert contextualization.

\subsection{Contributions of this paper}
This paper delivers a final, standalone specification by providing:
\begin{itemize}
    \item a formal decomposition model for inherited and residual semantic content,
    \item an estimation procedure with interpretability-preserving constraints,
    \item a verification and validation protocol for trustworthy use,
    \item and interpretation guardrails for responsible scientometric analysis.
\end{itemize}
